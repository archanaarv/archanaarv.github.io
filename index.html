About Me

About Me

I am a third-year Ph.D. candidate at the University of California, Riverside. I am an observational astronomer and I study black hole activities in small galaxies using Keck telescope. I am broadly interested in understanding the nature of low-mass black holes and the effect they have on their host dwarf galaxies. I work primarily with data in the visible and the near-infrared region of the electromagnetic spectrum. I am also interested in observational techniques such as Integral Field Spectroscopy and how to better use them to capture black hole activities in small galaxies.

Prior to my doctoral research, I did a five year Integrated Master of Science in Physics from Pondicherry University in India. For my undergraduate and masters research, I dabbled in Radio Astronomy, Astronomical Software, Solar Physics and Active Galactic Nuclei. I enjoyed working with AGNs for my Masters Thesis and continued to work on it for my Ph.D.

Apart from my research, I enjoy teaching, engaging in science communication. and doing astronomy outreach. I am also passionate about science policy and I hope to get more scientists involved in the government to make informed decisions on the policies that affect all of us.

Research

Research

Outflows in Dwarf galaxies

Gas outflows in dwarf galaxies play an important role in regulating feedback in dwarf galaxies and helping us to understand star formation in them. We study ionized gas outflows in dwarf galaxies to determine if there are any significant differences between the energetics of stellar-powered and AGN-powered outflows. We find that AGN-driven outflows are more energetic than stellar-powered outflows.

Detecting Intermediate-Mass Black Holes in Dwarf Galaxies using Variability

Detecting lower mass black holes in dwarf galaxies has always been a challenge, due to their weak signatures that often gets mixed with stars. We try to explore if mid-infrared variability is a better indicator of black holes, particularly in the lower-mass range.

Searching for signatures of strong nuclear activity using coronal lines

Coronal lines are forbidden lines that are caused by extremely energetic processes and are commonly believed to be an indicative signature of black holes. I use high resolution single slit spectroscopy as well as Integral Field Spectroscopy to search for such lines and identify potential signs of black holesin a low-metallicty galaxy J0944, commonly called as SB2.

Dual AGN

Detecting merging black holes observationally has been a challenge, as the galaxy at that stage witll be enshrouded in dust and gas. We use a novel technique that incorporates the variability of galaxies across different observational bands to detect dual merging AGNs using Integral field spectroscopy in the infrared.

Advocacy

Advocacy

Science has always inspired me. I have looked for ways to advocate for Science so that it can continue to inspire a lot more people. Here are some of the ways I try to achieve this

Science Outreach

I regularly help with designing and demonstrating affordable and easy-to-replicate Astronomy experiments for K-12 children as a part of Camp Highlander at UCR . I also help with telescope viewings for middle school students in the Riverside County.

Science Communication

I actively try to communicate Science to a more general audience to foster interest and make it more accessible. Check out my work for Astrobites, a collaboration of Astronomy graduate students who take scientific papers and condense them down into bite-sized pieces.


Listen to me talking about my research on black holes and why we need to have faith in Astrophysics as a podcast guest on Cosmic Voice a podcast by Spaceonova.

Science Policy

I am passionate about communicating science to policymakers and advocating for data-driven, evidence-based policy decisions through my involvement with the UCR Science to Policy group.


Check out my policy brief on Anticipatory Diplomacy in Outer Space, published by the Federation of American Scientists.

Science Equity

I am the current Vice-President of POWUR. Through this group, we hope to create a safe an inclusive space for womxn and minorities in the Physics and Astronomy department at UCR. I host monthy Womxn in Physics coffee socials and have run Book Clubs with high school Physics teachers to explore and discuss the relevance of STEM education to women.
